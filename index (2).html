01 — Market Opportunity

The most underserved market in mobility

Over 28% of American adults — more than 70 million people — live with some form of disability. Of those, 12% have serious mobility limitations. This is not a niche. It is one of the largest addressable markets in transportation, and it remains structurally underserved.

The opportunity is not just social — it is commercial. As Lyft's CEO noted, designing for older adults and disabled users opens up an entirely new segment of the boomer economy. Both companies are accelerating investment in this space, recognizing that accessible design benefits all users, not just those with disabilities.

🏥 B2B

Healthcare Transport

Non-emergency medical transport (NEMT) is a multi-billion dollar market. Uber Health has grown 300% year-over-year. Lyft Health is scaling fast through payer and broker partnerships.

👴 Consumer

Senior Independence

73 million Baby Boomers are entering their 70s. Seniors who lose driving ability still need mobility. Lyft Silver and Uber's simplified mode directly target this $84B+ senior care economy.

♿ Underserved

Wheelchair Access

Power wheelchair users have virtually no on-demand transport options outside major cities. WAV supply is the biggest product gap — and the biggest opportunity — in accessible mobility.

👨‍👩‍👧 Ecosystem

Caregiver Network

15% of Lyft riders are caregivers booking on behalf of others. 2 in 5 have ordered a ride for an older adult. The caregiver is an underutilized power user with high loyalty potential.

02 — User Segmentation

Who we are designing for

Accessibility is not a single user type. A strong PM answer segments by disability type, tech familiarity, and journey need — because the product solutions are fundamentally different for each group.

🦽

Wheelchair Users

Need WAV vehicles with ramps/locks. On-demand AND scheduled. Highest unmet need, clearest vehicle requirement. Primary design target.

👁️

Visual Impairments

Blind or low vision. Need screen reader compatibility, audio alerts, self-ID profiles, and communication preference settings with drivers.

👂

Deaf / Hard of Hearing

Need visual/vibration alerts, chat-preferred communication, flash notifications. Both driver and rider side of this equation need solving.

🧠

Cognitive / Developmental

IDD users need simplified interfaces, caregiver booking flows, and step-down complexity. Uber's "simple mode" and Lyft Silver directly address this.

👵

Senior Riders

Tech-hesitant, need larger UI, phone support, easier vehicle entry/exit. Fastest growing segment — the entire boomer population entering their 70s.

🤝

Caregivers

Book rides on behalf of others. Need delegation, live tracking, trusted contact sharing, and payment management. High frequency, high loyalty potential.

"Once older adults were familiar with the Lyft app, 86% opted to use it themselves — challenging the assumption that seniors need permanent caregiver assistance."
USC Study cited in Lyft Silver product development research, 2025
03 — Product Strategy Comparison

Two platforms. Two philosophies.

Uber and Lyft are solving the same problem with fundamentally different strategic bets. Understanding the difference is what separates a feature answer from a product strategy answer in any PM interview.

⬛ Uber — Scale & Technology First
UberWAV — largest global WAV network including London, NYC, D.C., LA, Delhi and more
Rider Self-ID (2024) — deaf, blind, service animal profiles with communication preferences
Uber Caregiver — delegate ride booking, payment, and tracking to a designated caregiver
Simple Mode / Senior Accounts — larger text, fewer buttons, streamlined interface launched mid-2025
Uber Health — 300% YoY growth, 1000+ healthcare organization partnerships for NEMT
Reserve WAV — advance booking up to 90 days for medical appointments (Austin pilot, expanding)
UberAssist — certified drivers trained for passengers needing extra boarding assistance
🩷 Lyft — Community & Partnership First
Lyft Silver (May 2025) — dedicated senior service: 1.4x fonts, phone support, easier-entry vehicles, nationwide
Lyft Assisted — drivers trained to help riders with boarding and exit, not just transport
Lyft Health / NEMT — scaling via payer and broker partnerships as a contracted B2B revenue stream
Transit agency partnerships — subsidized rides, voucher programs (N4 Connect: $160/month credits)
Freenow acquisition (2025) — European expansion adding regulated taxi and WAV networks across major cities
Concierge service — phone-based booking for users without smartphones, via nonprofit partners
Lyft Cash caregiver funding — family members can prepay rides for loved ones directly in-app

04 — AI Strategy

Who is betting on what

Both companies made landmark AI partnerships in 2024–2025. The strategic difference lies in where AI is being applied — and how close that application gets to the accessibility problem at its core.

⬛ Uber × OpenAI × NVIDIA

Scale & Operations AI

OpenAI Operator — AI-powered driver assistant, starting with EV adoption, expanding to broader support
Michelangelo ML platform — NLP-powered one-click chat smart replies; inadvertently helps deaf/HOH riders communicate faster
Uber AI Solutions — B2B AI data platform now in 30 countries; builds internal AI expertise that can be turned toward accessibility
NVIDIA for AV — Autonomous vehicle pipeline that could eliminate driver behavior as a barrier for disabled riders
Strategy: Build AI infrastructure → Accessibility benefits follow
🩷 Lyft × Anthropic × AWS × NVIDIA

Experience-First AI

Claude (Anthropic) — powers customer support with 87% faster resolution; eliminates confusing menu trees that disable cognitive users
Agentic AI via AWS — conversational support that understands natural language; 70% driver usage growth in 2025 shows behavior change is achievable
NVIDIA cuOpt + RAPIDS — AI-optimized driver-rider matching; improves WAV dispatch speed and reduces accessibility wait time deltas
Waymo & Baidu integration — Lyft as an AV aggregation platform; robotaxis provide barrier-free rides with no driver behavior risk
Strategy: Better experience today → AI removes barriers directly

Where AI goes next — the unbuilt opportunities

🔮

Predictive WAV Dispatch

Use ML to pre-position WAV vehicles by time and location — the same way surge pricing predicts demand. Reduces wait time from reactive to proactive.

🤖

Agentic Caregiver Booking

A voice/chat AI that caregivers can speak to naturally: "Book Maya's dialysis ride Thursday at 8am, she needs WAV." Full workflow handled end-to-end.

🎯

Adaptive UX Engine

Detect interaction patterns — slow taps, large text use, screen reader signals — and automatically adapt the UI. No manual settings page needed.

📚

Contextual Driver Training

Just-in-time AI training: when a driver accepts their first WAV ride, surface a 90-second module. Behavior change at the moment of need, not in a classroom.

06 — Metrics Framework

How to measure what matters

Great PMs don't just list metrics — they structure them by objective, key results, and guardrails. Here's how to frame accessibility metrics using the OKR → KPI hierarchy.

Objective
Make [Uber/Lyft] the most reliable, dignified mobility platform for disabled and senior riders in every city we operate.
Key Result 1

WAV ride request-to-completion rate reaches 90%+ in all supported cities within 12 months

Key Result 2

NPS for disabled and senior riders reaches within 10 points of platform-wide NPS within 12 months

Key Result 3

WAV average wait time within 20% of standard ride wait time in top 5 metro markets by Q3

⭐ North Star

% of disability-flagged ride requests successfully completed

Captures both supply availability and booking accessibility. The one number that tells you if the product is working for the people who need it most.

Acquisition

Disabled & senior user registrations per month

Track with segment tags — WAV, self-ID, caregiver, Silver. Growth signals both product-market fit and community trust.

Engagement

Repeat ride rate for accessible users

If disabled riders are coming back, the experience was dignified. If they're not, the product failed — regardless of completion rate.

Satisfaction

NPS & CSAT segmented by disability type

Overall NPS hides segment failures. A wheelchair user rating of 3 looks fine when averaged with 4.9-star rides. Disaggregate.

Guardrail

WAV wait time delta vs. standard rides

If the gap grows, supply is failing. This is the early warning signal before it becomes a rider experience problem.

Guardrail

Caregiver booking error & abandonment rate

Caregivers booking on behalf of others have zero tolerance for friction. A rising abandonment rate signals a UX failure in a critical flow.

07 — Interview Strategy

How a great PM answers this question

The difference between a good answer and a great answer in a product design interview is not more features. It's a clearer problem framing, a defensible prioritization, and metrics that show you understand what success actually looks like.

01

Start with the market, not the features

Open with: "1 in 7 people globally live with a disability. In the U.S. alone that's 70M+ adults — yet WAV rides exist in fewer than 10 cities on either major platform. The opportunity is massive and structurally underserved." This signals you understand the business case, not just the user need.

02

Name the real bottleneck: supply, not UX

Most candidates jump to app features. The insight that separates you: "We can perfect the booking experience, but if there are no WAV vehicles available, the product fails." Supply is the primary constraint. Your roadmap should reflect this.

03

Differentiate Uber vs. Lyft strategically

At Uber: design around global scale and driver behavior change. At Lyft: design around community partnerships and the institutional supply pipeline. Showing you understand each company's actual strategy makes your answer context-specific and memorable.

04

Use RICE — and defend your scores

Accessible App Experience scores 10.0. Accessible Fleet scores 4.5 — not because it's less important, but because effort is 5x higher. The insight is: "Start with the app because it unlocks every other initiative, and run fleet expansion in parallel."

05

Bring OKRs, not just a feature list

End with: Objective → Key Results → North Star metric. "% of disability-flagged requests successfully completed" as your North Star signals you're measuring outcomes, not outputs. This is the metric that tells you if disabled riders can actually use the platform.

06

Mention AI as infrastructure, not magic

Lyft's Anthropic partnership cut support resolution by 87% — that's real. The opportunity: agentic AI for caregiver booking, predictive WAV dispatch, and adaptive UX. Frame AI as the enabler of scale, not a standalone feature.

"The best PM answer to this question isn't a list of features. It's a supply strategy, a prioritization framework, and a metrics structure that makes the impact on real people visible and measurable."
