01 — The Case Against Generic Answers

Why most Uber accessibility case studies fail

Every PM candidate describes wheelchair ramps and screen readers. Nobody mentions the $125M DOJ lawsuit, the Lyft exec who said the strategy was to "do as little as possible unless forced," or the fact that WAV rides are available in fewer than 10 cities.

If you walk into an Uber or Lyft PM interview and describe both companies as "committed to accessibility," you will be filtered out. Interviewers at these companies live with the reality — the discrimination protests outside HQ, the lawsuits, the driver cancellations. A generic answer signals you haven't done homework.

The winning answer knows the gap between what companies say and what they do — and designs products that close it. This brief gives you everything you need to differentiate Uber and Lyft, reason about AI's role, and show PM-level product judgment.

The numbers neither company leads with

The uncomfortable truth: both companies built billion-dollar platforms and treated accessibility as a compliance checkbox. The Delta between marketing ("empowering independence!") and reality (fewer than 10 WAV cities, routine service animal denials) is the core product problem.

For a PM interview, this framing is gold. It lets you say: "The core problem isn't features — it's supply. We've built an accessible booking experience for a fleet that largely doesn't exist."

05 — With AI: Real Partnerships, Real Gaps

AI is not a feature. It's infrastructure.

Both companies made high-profile AI partnerships in 2024–2025. The gap is in what they're using AI for — and neither is using it to directly solve the disability access crisis.

Where AI should go next — the PM interview opportunity

Predictive WAV Dispatch

Use ML to predict WAV demand by time/location and pre-position drivers — same way Uber predicts surge. Neither company does this for accessibility. Highest-leverage AI application.

AI Cancellation Detection

Pattern-detect driver cancellation behavior correlated with disability flags. Auto-escalate with penalties. Uber's DOJ lawsuit is essentially a failure of this system. Train a classifier, enforce in real time.

Agentic Caregiver Booking

Lyft's Claude partnership is adjacent to this. An agentic AI that a caregiver can speak to naturally — "Book Maya's dialysis ride for Thursday at 8am, she needs WAV" — and handles the whole workflow.

Adaptive UX Engine

Detect user interaction patterns (slow taps, large text use, screen reader signals) and automatically adapt the UI. No manual settings page required. Reduces friction for cognitive disabilities.

Driver Training Personalization

Use AI to deliver contextual, just-in-time training: when a driver accepts their first WAV ride, show a 90-second module. When they accept a service animal ride, show the policy. Behavior change at the moment of need.

Compliance Monitoring

Build an ADA compliance dashboard for internal PMs — tracking cancellation rates, wait time deltas, and fee patterns by disability flag. This is the product that prevents $125M lawsuits.

06 — PM Interview Strategy

How to use this research in the room

The differentiation isn't "Uber has WAV and Lyft doesn't." It's in how each company's product philosophy, legal exposure, and AI strategy creates different design constraints — and opportunities.

"The question isn't 'how do we make the app more accessible?' It's 'how do we build a supply of accessible vehicles and trained drivers at the speed the community needs — and what does product do to make that happen?'"
The framing that separates a B+ from an A answer

If you're interviewing at Uber

Acknowledge the DOJ lawsuit — don't pretend it doesn't exist. Frame it as the product problem you're solving: "The gap between Uber's accessibility policy and driver behavior is a product failure, not just a training failure. How do we build systems that make compliant behavior the path of least resistance?" This shows you understand both the legal risk and the PM role in fixing it.

If you're interviewing at Lyft

Lean into the Anthropic/AI partnership and the Lyft Health/NEMT angle. Lyft has built institutional relationships that Uber hasn't — hospital networks, transit agencies, nonprofits. Your case study should design for that ecosystem: "How do we turn Lyft's community partnerships into a predictable WAV supply network?"

Guardrail metrics — don't forget these

Driver Cancellation Rate

Track specifically for trips where rider has disability flags set. A rising rate signals training failure or policy non-enforcement. The DOJ lawsuit is a proxy for this metric going unchecked.

WAV Wait Time Delta

The spread between standard and WAV wait times. If it grows, supply is failing. ADA requires "comparable" service — this metric is your legal compliance check.

Improper Fee Rate

% of disability-flagged rides resulting in contested wait fees or cleaning fees. A non-zero rate creates legal exposure. Both companies have paid settlements on this.

07 — Legal Context You Cannot Ignore

A decade of promises vs. courts

For a PM interview, knowing this timeline is how you show you understand the real product constraints. Both companies face regulatory pressure that shapes product roadmaps more than any internal prioritization framework.

2014
⬛ Uber

National Federation of the Blind sues Uber over guide dog ride denials. First sign that policies and driver behavior are misaligned.

2017
⬛ Uber

Equal Rights Center sues Uber — wheelchair accessible service deemed inadequate in D.C. Uber's platform design effectively excluded wheelchair users from UberX entirely.

2022
⬛ Uber

DOJ settlement: Uber agrees to waive wait-time fees for disabled riders. Must credit 65,000+ riders, pays $1.7M+ in compensation. Policy change — but enforcement remained inconsistent.

2024
🩷 Lyft

ADA class action Lowell v. Lyft goes to trial in July. Former Lyft WAV head's deposition quote — "do as little as possible unless forced" — becomes the most damaging phrase in rideshare accessibility history.

2024
Both

October: NFB protest outside Uber AND Lyft HQ in San Francisco over guide dog ride denials. Both companies respond with service animal self-ID features — reactive product development at its clearest.

Sept 2025
⬛ Uber

U.S. Department of Justice files $125M lawsuit against Uber — largest federal accessibility action against a rideshare company. Alleges routine ride denials, illegal surcharges, ADA non-compliance as a pattern and practice. Uber denies all allegations.

08 — The Ruthless Verdict

Neither company deserves the title. Here's who's closer.

If you had to design an accessible rideshare from scratch in 2026, you'd take Lyft's community model, Uber's global scale, and neither company's approach to supply-side investment.

Uber's honest card

Best at: App sophistication, global WAV reach, institutional partnerships, and rider self-ID. Has the scale to fix the supply problem if regulatory pressure forces it.

Honest failure: A decade of lawsuits with no fundamental behavior change. $125M DOJ exposure is a product failure, not just a legal one. The pattern — protest, settle, promise, repeat — is now documented.

If it works: AI-powered cancellation detection + mandatory driver compliance + financial penalties for ADA violations could make Uber the category leader within 2 years. The ingredients exist. The will hasn't.

Lyft's honest card

Best at: Community trust, institutional health partnerships (Lyft Health/NEMT), AI-powered support quality, and senior-specific product design (Lyft Silver). Anthropic partnership is the most credible AI accessibility move in the industry.

Honest failure: Smaller WAV network, reactive strategy, and an internal culture caught on record treating accessibility as burden rather than opportunity. WAV in 9 cities for a national platform is indefensible.

If it works: Lyft's institutional relationships with hospital systems and transit agencies are an underused supply lever. If they build a Lyft Health → Lyft Wheelchair pipeline, they could leapfrog Uber on actual disability access outcomes.

"The best PM answer to 'design an accessible rideshare' is not a list of features. It's a supply strategy, an enforcement mechanism, and a metrics framework that makes discrimination visible in real time."
